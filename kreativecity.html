Kreative City

The Goal

Kreative City is a native app for iOS and Android that functions as a virtual marketplace to connect artists and art enthusiasts. Kreative City helps consumers easily find and buy local art and support artists and small businesses.

My role

As the sole designer, I was responsible for both the UX and the UI for this project, with a stronger focus on the UI.

The Tools

  • Sketch
  • InVision

The Result

View Android Prototype

Designing for two user groups

This project had a heavier focus on the UI than on the UX, and for that reason, I dove right into user stories, making sure to include the needs of both buyers and sellers. Like with Depop and Poshmark, everyone should be able to use the app for both buying and selling. Based on these user stories, I created simple user flows.

Digital natives

Next, I sketched some quick, preliminary wireframes for key screens for both iOS and Android. The user flows were the same for both versions, but some wireframes differed according to the guidelines. Below are a few wireframes for iOS.

The next step was refining the wireframes by analyzing similar apps and digging deeper into the iOS human interface and Android material guidelines. For each of these steps, I went through another couple rounds of wireframing, drawing upon inspiration from other apps and refining for Android and iOS.

Defining my target audience

The analysis of other apps was especially useful for defining my target audience. While Depop, with its Instagram aesthetics, has a younger audience base, itâ€™s geared specifically for people to sell secondhand clothing, although some have co-opted it to sell their art. Etsy, on the other hand, was specifically created for selling art, but its user base is generally older. Poshmark also seems aimed at an older audience, with its department store style menu and more traditional online store layout. This app should fill the gap and give a younger user base the perfect place to buy and sell art.

Sound & Haptic

Because I was focusing on the look and feel of the app, I dove into sound and haptic gestures to ensure that every interaction was smooth, harmonious, and aligned with the Android and iOS guidelines. For this reason, I digitized my new round of wireframes and annotated them for sound and haptic gestures, according to their respective native guidelines.

Tracking changes

Next I established a style guide for each set of wireframes in alignment with their respective guidelines and applied it. Again, because I was focusing in particular on the UI for this project, I submitted my initial screens for critique from fellow designers, rather than moving straight to user testing. I made sure to have Android users give feedback on the Android version, and Apple users give feedback on the iOS version. Below are a few of the initial screens I presented for critique.

For this project, I compiled the feedback I received in one place, but as I applied the changes, I deleted each piece of feedback. While it was satisfying to see my progress visually, I regret not documenting these changes for later. Nor did I save an older version of the document, but instead overwrote the old one. For one thing, keeping a repository of changes can be useful if you ever need to go back to the old version. But it's also incredibly useful for documenting your case studies and keeping track of your own progress. This was an important lesson, and since then, I've been careful to keep a record of what I've changed.

Conclusion

This project was a fantastic way to dive headfirst into native design and begin to look at key differences between the two. I learned a lot about how to design for each one, and what kinds of things you need to consider for each. I also learned an important lesson about tracking the feedback you receive and the changes you make. Because UX design is such an iterative process, it's especially important to keep a record of what changes you made and why.

I also learned that I enjoy a project more when I can work on the process from start to finish. Although I love working on the UI, end-to-end design provides a much deeper, richer understanding of the problem space. Besides, being able to empathize more closely with users makes it that much more satisfying to find a solution that meets their needs.
